## Grammatical Analysis

-   **Present Tense Narration:** The entire text uses the simple present tense (`quita`, `es`, `debe`, `está`, `dice`, `da`, `gusta`, `quiere`, `siguen queriendo`) to narrate events, which is a common technique in news reporting to make the information feel more immediate.
-   **Reflexive Verbs:** `irse` (to go away, to leave) is a reflexive verb that indicates the action of leaving a place. It's a more definitive "leaving" than just `ir` (to go).
-   **"volver a" + Infinitive:** The phrase `vuelva a quedar bien` means "to look good *again*". The structure "volver a + [infinitive]" is a common way to express the repetition of an action.
-   **Subjunctive Mood:** `haga` is in the present subjunctive. It's used here because it follows a verb of desire/want (`queriendo que...`). The structure is `[verb of influence] + que + [different subject] + [verb in subjunctive]`.

### Grammar Analysis

-   **Pretérito Indefinido (Simple Past):** `retiró` (withdrew), `tuvo` (had), `ocurrió` (happened), `hizo` (did/made), `dijo` (said), `tenía` (had), `pagó` (paid). Used for completed actions in the past.
-   **Imperfect Tense:** `era` (was), `tenía` (had). Used to describe past states, conditions, or ongoing actions in the past.
-   **"ya no se llama"**: "is no longer called." The reflexive "se" is used here for "to be called." "Ya no" means "no longer."
-   **"tuvo que" + Infinitive:** "had to" + infinitive. Expresses obligation in the past.
-   **"hizo daño a"**: "harmed." A common phrase meaning "to cause harm to."
-   **"cuando tenía 17 años"**: "when she was 17 years old." The imperfect tense is used for age in the past.
-   **"poner fin a"**: "to put an end to." A common phrase.
-   **"hizo lo correcto"**: "did the right thing." "Lo correcto" (the correct thing) uses the neuter article "lo."
-   **"siguen queriendo"**: "still want." "Seguir" + gerund means "to continue doing something."
-   **"siguen apoyando"**: "continue supporting." Another example of "seguir" + gerund.
-   **"Aunque ahora a la mayoría de la gente no le guste"**: "Although most people don't like him now." Uses the subjunctive `guste` after "aunque" (although) when expressing a hypothetical or contrary-to-fact situation, or a subjective opinion.

## Deeper Grammatical Analysis

Here is a more detailed look at some of the clauses and phrases from the last article:

-   **`...que abandone su lujosa residencia...`**: This is a subordinate noun clause acting as the direct object of the verb `ha ordenado` (has ordered). The verb `abandone` is in the present subjunctive because it follows a verb of command (`ordenar que`).

-   **`...para distanciar a la monarquía...`**: This is an adverbial clause of purpose. The preposition `para` followed by an infinitive (`distanciar`) is a common way to express "in order to..." or "so as to...".

-   **`...relacionados con Jeffrey Epstein...`**: Here, `relacionados` is a past participle used as an adjective modifying `escándalos`. It agrees in gender and number with the noun it modifies.

-   **`...cuando ella tenía 17 años...`**: This is a subordinate adverbial clause of time. It specifies *when* the main action (`acusó a Andrés`) occurred. The imperfect tense (`tenía`) is used to describe her age, which is a state or condition in the past.

-   **`...aunque él negó las acusaciones...`**: This is a concessive clause. It uses `aunque` (although/even though) followed by the indicative (`negó`) because it is stating a known fact: he did, in fact, deny the accusations.

-   **`...sin admitir su culpabilidad...`**: This is a prepositional phrase expressing manner. The preposition `sin` (without) is followed by an infinitive (`admitir`), which is standard in Spanish.

-   **`...sostienen que perder sus títulos no es suficiente...`**: The clause `que perder sus títulos no es suficiente` is a noun clause. It functions as the direct object of the verb `sostienen` (they argue). The subject of this clause is the infinitive phrase `perder sus títulos` (losing his titles).
