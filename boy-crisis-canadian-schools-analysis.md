# Analysis of the Boy Crisis in Canadian Schools

## 1. Original Spanish Text

Los expertos están cada vez más preocupados por la «crisis de los chicos» en las escuelas de Canadá y de todo el mundo.

Los datos muestran que los chicos se están quedando atrás respecto a las chicas en varios ámbitos clave. En Quebec, por ejemplo, la tasa de abandono escolar entre los chicos supera el 27 %, frente a alrededor del 20 % entre las chicas.

Los chicos también tienen menos probabilidades de ir a la universidad; actualmente, solo alrededor del 44 % de los estudiantes de educación superior en Canadá son hombres. Esta brecha existe en parte porque las aulas tradicionales suelen valorar el estar quieto, el orden y seguir normas estrictas, tareas que a muchos chicos jóvenes les resultan difíciles. Las consecuencias de esta brecha son graves. Cuando los chicos sienten que no pueden tener éxito en la escuela, a menudo se desmotivan o se rinden. Esto puede llevar a problemas de salud mental, a una falta de oportunidades laborales y a un mayor riesgo de caer en influencias tóxicas en Internet.

Algunos profesores sugieren que la forma en que evaluamos a los alumnos debería cambiar para incluir la organización activa, como el trabajo en equipo que se ve durante los deportes en el recreo. Para solucionar el problema, los expertos sugieren más actividad física y entornos de aprendizaje flexibles que ayuden a los chicos a sentirse integrados sin perjudicar el progreso de las chicas.

## 2. English Translation

Experts are increasingly concerned about the "boy crisis" in schools in Canada and around the world.

The data shows that boys are falling behind girls in several key areas. In Quebec, for example, the school dropout rate among boys exceeds 27%, compared to around 20% among girls.

Boys are also less likely to go to university; currently, only about 44% of higher education students in Canada are men. This gap exists in part because traditional classrooms tend to value staying still, order, and following strict rules—tasks that many young boys find difficult. The consequences of this gap are serious. When boys feel they cannot succeed in school, they often become demotivated or give up. This can lead to mental health problems, a lack of job opportunities, and an increased risk of falling into toxic influences on the internet.

Some teachers suggest that the way we evaluate students should change to include active organization, such as the teamwork seen during sports at recess. To address the problem, experts suggest more physical activity and flexible learning environments that help boys feel included without harming girls' progress.

## 4. Grammar Analysis

### Key Grammar Patterns

**1. Stem-changing verb *soler* (to tend to)**
> "las aulas tradicionales **suelen valorar** el estar quieto..."
*soler* is a defective verb used only in present and imperfect tenses. It expresses habitual action or tendency, equivalent to English "tend to" or "usually." Here, *suelen valorar* = "they tend to value."

**2. Reflexive verbs for state changes**
> "a menudo se **desmotivan** o se **rinden**"
Reflexive verbs in Spanish often indicate self-directed action or involuntary state changes. *desmotivarse* and *rendirse* use the *se* reflexive pronoun to show the boys are affected by the action.

**3. Verb *resultar* + adjective = "prove to be"**
> "tareas que a muchos chicos jóvenes les **resultan difíciles**"
*resultar* followed by an adjective means "to prove to be" or "to turn out to be." The construction *resultar difícil* = "to be difficult."

**4. *Poder* + infinitive for possibility**
> "Esto puede **llevar a** problemas de salud mental"
*poder* (can/may) + infinitive expresses possibility. Note *puede llevar* not *puede ser llevado* — the active form is standard.

**5. *Debería* + infinitive for suggestion**
> "la forma en que evaluamos a los alumnos debería **cambiar**"
*debería* (conditional of *deber*) softens obligation into suggestion — "should" rather than "must."

**6. Prepositions *a* and *en* with infinitive phrases**
> "sin **perjudicar** el progreso de las chicas"
*sin* + infinitive creates a concomitant circumstance clause — "without harming." This is a common pattern in formal/academic Spanish.

**7. Comparative structures**
> "menos probabilidades," "mayor riesgo," "más actividad física"
Spanish comparatives use *más...que* (more...than), *menos...que* (less...than), and adjectivecomparisons like *mayor* (greater), *menor* (smaller).

## 5. Idioms and Expressions

- **quedan atrás** — "are falling behind" (literally "remain behind"); used to describe being outperformed in metrics.
- **tasa de abandono escolar** — "school dropout rate"; *abandono escolar* is the standard term for students who leave school before completion.
- **brecha** (de género) — "gender gap"; commonly used in discussions of disparity (*brecha salarial* = pay gap, *brecha digital* = digital divide).
- **tener éxito** — "to succeed"; literally "to have success" — a common collocation: *tener éxito en algo* = "to be successful at something."
- **caer en influencias tóxicas** — "to fall into toxic influences"; *caer en* implies being drawn into something negative, like falling into a trap.
- **mayor riesgo de caer en** — "increased risk of falling into"; *riesgo de + infinitive* or *riesgo de + noun* is the standard pattern.
- **a la orden** — "at your service"; though not in this text, note that *orden* in the command sense appears in phrases like *a la orden* in service contexts.
- **sentirse integrado** — "to feel included/integrated"; *sentirse* + past participle is common for describing states of being.
