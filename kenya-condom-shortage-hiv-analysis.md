# Analysis of Kenya's Condom Shortage and HIV Progress

## 1. Original Spanish Text

Kenia ha logrado grandes avances en la lucha contra el VIH en los últimos diez años y ha reducido considerablemente el número de nuevas infecciones. Sin embargo, este éxito está ahora en peligro porque al país se le están acabando los preservativos gratuitos.

Más de la mitad de estos condones solían proceder de donaciones internacionales, especialmente de Estados Unidos, pero Washington ha recortado drásticamente su ayuda exterior. Este cambio deja a los grupos vulnerables, como las trabajadoras sexuales, en una situación difícil porque no pueden permitirse comprar protección. Activistas y expertos advierten de que, sin suministros gratuitos, Kenia podría volver a la situación crítica de los años 80 y 90.

El problema se agrava por los elevados impuestos locales que gravan incluso la importación de preservativos gratuitos, lo que encarece demasiado su distribución local. Además, los trabajadores locales informan de un fuerte aumento de las infecciones de transmisión sexual y de los embarazos adolescentes entre los jóvenes, que constituyen la mayoría de la población sexualmente activa.

## 2. English Translation

Kenya has made great progress in the fight against HIV in the last ten years and has considerably reduced the number of new infections. However, this success is now in danger because the country is running out of free condoms.

More than half of these condoms used to come from international donations, especially from the United States, but Washington has drastically cut its foreign aid. This change leaves vulnerable groups, such as sex workers, in a difficult situation because they cannot afford to buy protection. Activists and experts warn that without free supplies, Kenya could return to the critical situation of the 1980s and 1990s.

The problem is worsened by elevated local taxes that even tax the import of free condoms, which makes their local distribution too expensive. Additionally, local workers report a sharp increase in sexually transmitted infections and teenage pregnancies among young people, who make up the majority of the sexually active population.

## 4. Grammar Analysis

### Present Perfect with hacer-Periphrase

The article opens with: *Kenia ha logrado...y ha reducido...*

This is a standard present perfect construction (*haber* + past participle), used here to describe completed actions with present relevance — Kenya's achievements over the last ten years still matter now. The time expression *en los últimos diez años* (in the last ten years) anchors it as a recent-past-with-ongoing-effect context, which is exactly when present perfect is appropriate in Spanish.

### Se-Leaving Construction (Se-Leísmo)

*"...porque al país se le están acabando los preservativos."*

This is the famous **se-le-le** (or **se-le-lo**) construction, sometimes called a "se-leiving" construction in English. The reflexive verb *acabarse* (to run out of) is combined with an indirect object pronoun (*le* → *se* before *está*) to indicate that the running out happens **to** the country:

- *se le están acabando* = "they are ending to it" = "it is running out of"
- *al país* is the explicit indirect object (the preposition *a* marks it as recipient)

This is a very Spanish construction and one of the most challenging grammatical features for learners.

### Soler + Infinitive (Habitual Past)

*"...estos condones solían proceder de donaciones internacionales..."*

The verb *soler* (to be accustomed to, to usually do) in the imperfect tense (*solían*) followed by an infinitive (*proceder*) expresses a habitual past action — what used to happen. This is different from simple imperfect (*procedían*), which would just describe an ongoing action in the past without the sense of habitual custom.

### Passive Construction with Se (Pasiva Refleja)

*"...que gravan incluso la importación de preservativos gratuitos..."*

Here, *gravar* (to tax) is in 3rd person plural (*gavan*), which in this context functions as an **impersonal/reflexive passive**: "they tax" but understood as a general, unspecified subject ("taxes are levied"). The construction follows the pattern:

**se + third person singular/plural verb + noun**
- *Se habla español* = Spanish is spoken
- *grava...la importación* = the import is taxed / they tax the import

### Phrase: Advertir de que

*"Activistas y expertos advierten de que, sin suministros gratuitos, Kenia podría volver..."*

*Advertir* (to warn) takes the preposition *de* before its object. So *advierten de que* = "they warn that." Note the structure: *advertir de algo/a alguien* (warn about something/to someone) and *advertir de que* (warn that).

Also note: *podría volver* — the conditional of *poder* (*podría* = could/might) expresses possibility here, not direct ability.

### Relative Clause with lo que

*"...lo que encarece demasiado su distribución local."*

*Lo que* here is a **relative pronoun** meaning "which" (referring to the entire previous clause). Literally: "which makes their local distribution too expensive." This is a very common Spanish construction for making commentary about a preceding fact.

Compare:
- *lo que pasa* = what happens / the thing that happens
- *lo que dice* = what he/she says
- *lo que encareció* = which made it expensive (referring back to a situation)

### Conjunction Chain

*"...un fuerte aumento de las infecciones de transmisión sexual y de los embarazos adolescentes entre los jóvenes, **que** constituyen la mayoría de la población sexualmente activa."*

Note the double **de** before *las infecciones* and *los embarazos adolescentes*. This is because *aumento* takes *de* (*aumento de* = increase of), and then the noun phrase is built: *aumento de [las infecciones de transmisión sexual] y [los embarazos adolescentes]*. The phrase *entre los jóvenes* adds another layer: "among young people."

The final *que* introduces a relative clause: *que constituyen* = "who/which constitute" — describing *los jóvenes*.

## 5. Idioms and Expressions

- **"lograr grandes avances"** — "to make great strides / achieve major progress." *Lograr* means to achieve or attain; *avances* are advances. Common in progress/reform contexts.

- **"estar en peligro"** — "to be in danger / at risk." *Poner en peligro* = to put at risk; *correr peligro* = to be in peril.

- **"acabarse"** (reflexive) — "to run out / to be used up." *Se me acabó el tiempo* = "I ran out of time." Unlike *terminar* (to end), *acabarse* emphasizes depletion.

- **"no poder permitirse"** — "cannot afford." *Permitirse* as a reflexive verb with this meaning: *no puedo permitirme ese lujo* = I can't afford that luxury. Here: *no pueden permitirse comprar protección* = they can't afford to buy protection.

- **"advertir de que"** — "to warn that." *Advertir* carries a sense of giving advance notice or cautionary advice. *Prevenga* is similar but more forceful.

- **"volver a la situación crítica"** — "to return to the critical situation." The construction *volver a + [noun]* means to return to a state: *volver al trabajo* (return to work), *volver a casa* (return home).

- **"el problema se agrava"** — "the problem worsens." *Agravarse* means to get worse or more serious, distinct from *empeorar* (to worsen) or *empeorar* is also used.

- **"constituir la mayoría"** — "to make up the majority." *Constituir* = to constitute, to form, to represent. Common in demographic/statistical contexts.

- **"población sexualmente activa"** — "sexually active population." A calque from English, commonly used in health and demographic reporting across Spanish-speaking countries.
