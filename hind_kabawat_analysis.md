## Original Spanish Text

Hind Kabawat es la ministra de Asuntos Sociales y Trabajo de Siria y la única mujer del Gobierno de transición del país. Ha hablado abiertamente de su preocupación por la falta de mujeres en el poder y ha afirmado que esto fue un error al inicio de la transición. Su trabajo incluye visitar campamentos donde las familias viven en condiciones precarias y con poca ayuda. La violencia entre grupos ha minado la confianza en los nuevos líderes. Kabawat pide paciencia y solicita diálogo y respeto durante el difícil camino de Siria hacia la paz.

## English Translation

Hind Kabawat is the Minister of Social Affairs and Labor of Syria and the only woman in the country's transitional government. She has spoken openly about her concern over the lack of women in power and has stated that this was a mistake at the beginning of the transition. After years of war and dictatorship, the new government must demonstrate change and responsibility.

Kabawat lived in exile for many years and now works with many women in her ministry. She states that she does not see herself as a symbol, but as someone who serves the people. She rejects the idea that she is only there to look good and affirms that she needs real power, plans, and money to help people.

Her work includes visiting camps where families live in precarious conditions and with little help. The violence between groups has undermined trust in the new leaders. Kabawat asks for patience and requests dialogue and respect during Syria's difficult path to peace.

***Note on a typo in the original text:** The phrase "en tu ministerio" (in your ministry) was translated as "in her ministry," correcting the likely typo from "tu" (informal 'your') to "su" (her/his/their/formal 'your'), which fits the context of the article.*

## Grammar and Syntax Analysis

*   **Present Perfect (Pretérito Perfecto Compuesto):** The text uses this tense (e.g., `ha hablado`, `ha afirmado`, `ha minado`) to describe past actions that are recent or have a direct relevance to the present situation. For example, "She has spoken openly" implies this is a continuing stance.
*   **Simple Past (Pretérito Indefinido):** Used for completed, finished actions in the past, such as `vivió` (she lived) and `fue` (it was). These actions are contained within a specific past timeframe.
*   **Present Tense (Presente):** The majority of the verbs are in the present tense (`es`, `trabaja`, `afirma`, `rechaza`, `necesita`, `pide`) to describe the current situation, Kabawat's current role, and her ongoing beliefs.
*   **Subjunctive Mood:** The phrase `rechaza la idea de que solo **esté** ahí` uses the present subjunctive (`esté`). This is triggered by the expression of rejection/doubt (`rechaza la idea de que...`), indicating the following clause is a subjective or hypothetical concept, not a declared fact.
*   **Verb `deber` + Infinitive:** The phrase `debe demostrar` (must demonstrate) is used to express obligation or necessity.

## Idioms and Expressions

*   **Hablar abiertamente:** Literally "to speak openly." This is a common expression equivalent to the English "to speak frankly" or "to be outspoken."
*   **En el poder:** A direct equivalent to the English political phrase "in power."
*   **Quedar bien:** This is a nuanced idiom. While it can mean "to look good," it often carries the connotation of doing something for the sake of appearances or to make a good impression, rather than from genuine conviction.
*   **Verse a sí misma como:** "To see oneself as." A standard reflexive construction used to talk about self-perception.
*   **Camino hacia la paz:** "Path to peace." A common metaphor used in both Spanish and English to describe the process of achieving peace.
