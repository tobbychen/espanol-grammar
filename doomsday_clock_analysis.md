# Analysis of the Doomsday Clock Text

## 1. Original Spanish Text (Texto Original)

Los científicos atómicos acercan el Reloj del Juicio Final a la medianoche más que nunca. El reloj marca ahora ochenta y cinco segundos para la medianoche, lo que indica un peligro extremo para el mundo. Los expertos afirman que este peligro proviene de las armas nucleares, el débil control global, las guerras, los problemas climáticos y el rápido crecimiento de la inteligencia artificial.

Muchas personas ven el reloj como una clara advertencia de lo cerca que está el mundo del desastre. Los científicos señalan las acciones agresivas de Estados Unidos, Rusia y China. Les preocupan las amenazas nucleares, los débiles acuerdos sobre armas y las guerras en curso en Ucrania y Oriente Medio. También temen el uso de la inteligencia artificial en el ámbito militar y la difusión de información falsa. El cambio climático añade más presión a una situación ya de por sí inestable.

Los expertos afirman que los líderes mundiales no colaboran entre sí ni protegen la paz. Los acuerdos nucleares se rompen y la confianza entre los países sigue disminuyendo. Los científicos advierten de que, sin una cooperación sólida y unas normas claras, el riesgo de catástrofe mundial seguirá aumentando.

## 2. English Translation (Traducción al Inglés)

Atomic scientists move the Doomsday Clock closer to midnight than ever before. The clock now reads eighty-five seconds to midnight, which indicates extreme danger for the world. Experts state that this danger comes from nuclear weapons, weak global control, wars, climate problems, and the rapid growth of artificial intelligence.

Many people see the clock as a clear warning of how close the world is to disaster. Scientists point to the aggressive actions of the United States, Russia, and China. They are concerned about nuclear threats, weak arms agreements, and the ongoing wars in Ukraine and the Middle East. They also fear the use of artificial intelligence in the military and the spread of false information. Climate change adds more pressure to an already unstable situation.

Experts state that world leaders are not collaborating with each other nor protecting peace. Nuclear agreements are breaking down, and trust between countries continues to decrease. Scientists warn that without strong cooperation and clear rules, the risk of global catastrophe will continue to increase.

## 4. Grammar and Structures (Gramática y Estructuras)

This text uses clear, journalistic language. Key grammatical points include:

*   **Present Tense for Reporting**: The simple present tense is used throughout to report current events and state facts (e.g., `acercan`, `marca`, `afirman`, `ven`). This is the standard tense for news reporting.
*   **`lo que`**: This relative pronoun means "which" and refers to a previously stated idea or situation.
    *   **Example**: "...ochenta y cinco segundos para la medianoche, **lo que indica** un peligro extremo..." (...eighty-five seconds to midnight, **which indicates** extreme danger...).
*   **`cerca que está`**: This structure is used to express "how close it is." The pattern `[adjective] + que + [verb]` is a common way to form exclamations or statements about degree.
    *   **Example**: "...de lo **cerca que está** el mundo del desastre." (...of **how close** the world **is** to disaster.).
*   **Passive Voice with `se`**: The pronoun `se` is used to form a passive-like statement, where the action is happening to the subject without specifying who is performing the action.
    *   **Example**: "Los acuerdos nucleares **se rompen**..." (Nuclear agreements **are breaking down** / are being broken...).
*   **Verbs like `gustar`**: The verb `preocupar` (to worry) is used in a structure similar to `gustar`. The thing that causes the worry is the subject of the sentence.
    *   **Example**: "**Les preocupan** las amenazas nucleares..." (Literally: "Nuclear threats worry them," meaning "**They are concerned about** nuclear threats...").
*   **Future Tense `(futuro simple)`**: The future tense is used to make a prediction about what will happen if current conditions persist.
    *   **Example**: "...el riesgo... **seguirá aumentando**." (...the risk... **will continue to increase**.).

## 5. Idioms and Expressions (Modismos y Expresiones)

The text is formal and direct, so it contains few true idioms. However, some phrases are worth noting:

*   **`más que nunca`**: A very common expression meaning "more than ever."
*   **`en curso`**: Means "ongoing" or "in progress." It's frequently used in formal and news contexts to describe events that have not concluded.
    *   **Example**: `guerras en curso` (ongoing wars).
*   **`de por sí`**: This phrase means "in itself," "as it is," or "already." It's used to emphasize an inherent quality of something.
    *   **Example**: "...a una situación ya **de por sí** inestable." (...to an **already** unstable situation.).
*   **`marcar la hora`**: Literally "to mark the hour," this is the standard way to say "to tell the time." Here, `el reloj marca` means "the clock reads" or "the clock indicates."
