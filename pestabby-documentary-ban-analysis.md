# Analysis of Indonesia's "Pestabby" Documentary Ban

## 1. Original Spanish Text

Las autoridades indonesias están impidiendo que la gente vea una nueva película llamada «Pestabby». La película muestra cómo el gobierno expropia las tierras de la población local en Papúa.

El gobierno quiere talar grandes bosques para construir grandes explotaciones agrícolas destinadas al cultivo de arroz y azúcar. Sin embargo, estos bosques son el hogar de los indígenas papúes, que dependen de ellos para sobrevivir, y allí se encuentran sus lugares sagrados. Desde que se estrenó la película, los realizadores han recibido graves amenazas y la policía secreta los ha estado vigilando. El ejército detuvo las proyecciones en las universidades porque, según dijeron, el tema era demasiado delicado y podía causar problemas. Papúa sigue siendo un tema políticamente delicado, y el gobierno indonesio limita estrictamente el acceso a la región para los medios internacionales y los grupos de derechos humanos. Pero como el gobierno intentó prohibir la película, esta se hizo muy famosa.

Los intentos del gobierno de prohibir el documental han generado una publicidad masiva, lo que ha llevado a que se agoten las entradas en las proyecciones por todo el país.

## 2. English Translation

Indonesian authorities are preventing people from seeing a new film called "Pestabby." The film shows how the government expropriates the lands of the local population in Papua.

The government wants to fell large forests to build large agricultural operations destined for rice and sugar cultivation. However, these forests are the home of the Papuan indigenous people, who depend on them for survival, and there are their sacred places located there. Since the film premiered, the filmmakers have received serious threats and the secret police have been watching them. The army halted the screenings at universities because, according to them, the topic was too delicate and could cause problems. Papua remains a politically delicate topic, and the Indonesian government strictly limits access to the region for international media and human rights groups. But because the government tried to ban the film, it became very famous.

The government's attempts to ban the documentary have generated massive publicity, which has led to sold-out screenings across the country.

## 3. Glossary

- **autoridades indonesias** — feminine plural noun phrase. "Indonesian authorities."
- **impidiendo** — verb, present participle of *impedir* (to prevent, to hinder). Gerund form.
- **Pestabby** — proper noun. Title of the documentary film.
- **expropia** — verb, 3rd person singular present indicative of *expropiar* (to expropriate, to seize).
- **tierras de la población local** — feminine plural noun + prepositional phrase. "Lands of the local population."
- **talar** — verb, infinitive. "To fell, to cut down (trees)."
- **explotaciones agrícolas** — feminine plural noun + feminine plural adjective. "Agricultural operations/farms."
- **destinadas al cultivo de** — feminine plural adjective + prepositional phrase. "Destined for the cultivation of."
- **arroz** — masculine noun. "Rice."
- **azúcar** — masculine noun. "Sugar."
- **hogar** — masculine noun. "Home." Here used in plural: *son el hogar* = "are the home."
- **indígenas papúes** — adjective/noun, common gender plural. "Papuan indigenous (people)."
- **sobreviver** — verb, infinitive. "To survive."
- **lugares sagrados** — masculine plural noun + masculine plural adjective. "Sacared places."
- **estreno** — masculine noun. "Premiere, debut." *Desde que se estr* = "Since it premiered."
- **realizadores** — masculine plural noun. "Filmmakers, directors."
- **amenazas** — feminine plural noun. "Threats."
- **policía secreta** — feminine noun + feminine adjective. "Secret police."
- **ha estado vigilando** — verb phrase, present perfect continuous of *vigilar* (to watch). "Has been watching."
- **proyecciones** — feminine plural noun. "Screenings, projections."
- **delicado** — adjective. "Delicate, sensitive."
- **medios internacionales** — masculine plural noun. "International media."
- **grupos de derechos humanos** — masculine plural noun + prepositional phrase. "Human rights groups."
- **intentar prohibir** — verb phrase. "To try to ban."
- **publicidad masiva** — feminine noun + feminine adjective. "Massive publicity."
- **se agoten** — verb, present subjunctive 3rd person plural of *agotarse* (to run out, to be exhausted). Passive/reflexive construction.
- **entradas** — feminine plural noun. "Tickets."
- **por todo el país** — prepositional phrase. "Across the whole country."

## 4. Grammar Analysis

### Present Progressive with "estar + -ndo"

**"Las autoridades indonesias están impidiendo que la gente vea..."** uses the present progressive *estar + gerund* to describe an ongoing action:
- *están impidiendo* (progressive: "are preventing")
- *impiden* (simple present: "prevent")

The gerund form *impidiendo* comes from *impedir* (e→i stem change in stressed syllables).

### Subjunctive After Verbs of Preventing/Impeding

**"están impidiendo que la gente vea"** — The verb *impedir* (to prevent) takes the subjunctive in its subordinate clause. The structure is: *impedir que + subjunctive*. The subjunctive mood signals that the action in the subordinate clause is uncertain or dependent on the main verb's influence.

### Passive Construction with "se"

**"desde que se estr* la película"** — The *se* passive (pasiva refleja) is used: *seizó* = "it was premiered" or "it premiered." This construction is common when the agent is unknown or unimportant.

### Present Perfect Continuous

**"la policía secreta los ha estado vigilando"** — This is the present perfect progressive: *haber estado + gerund*. It emphasizes the continuation of an action that began in the past and continues to the present.

### Por vs. Para

**"destinadas al cultivo de"** uses *para* (not *por*) because it indicates purpose/destination — "destined *for* cultivation."

**"por todo el país"** uses *por* because it indicates location/movement — "*across* the whole country."

### Relative Clause with "que"

**"los bosques que son el hogar"** — The relative pronoun *que* connects the subordinate clause to its antecedent *bosques*. Note that *que* is used for both people and things in Spanish (unlike English which uses "who/which/that").

### Porque vs. Como

**"Como el gobierno intentó prohibir la película..."** — Here *como* means "because/since" (not "how"). It introduces a causal clause.

### Reflexive "se agoten" in Subjunctive Context

**"lo que ha llevado a que se agoten las entradas"** — The phrase *llevar a que* + subjunctive expresses "has led to (something happening)." The reflexive *se agoten* makes the verb passive: "the tickets are exhausted/sold out."

### "Tierra(s)" as Uncountable Noun

**"las tierras de la población local"** — Here *tierras* means "lands/territories" (not "soils"). In this context, it refers to land ownership and territory rights.

## 5. Idioms and Expressions

- **impedir que** — "to prevent someone from (doing something)." *Están impidiendo que la gente vea* = "They are preventing people from seeing." Takes subjunctive in the subordinate clause.

- **desde que** — "since" (temporal conjunction). *Desde que se estr* = "Since it premiered."

- **hacer(se) famoso/a** — "to become famous." *Esta se hizo muy famosa* = "It became very famous."

- **intentar + infinitive** — "to try to (do something)." *Intentar prohibir* = "to try to ban." Note the difference from English which uses the gerund after "try."

- **dar lugar a que** — "to lead to, to result in." *Lo que ha llevado a que se agoten* = "which has led to (them) selling out."

- **por todo el país** — "across the whole country." A common phrase for nation-wide distribution.

- **el tema era demasiado delicado** — "the topic was too delicate." *Demasiado + adjective* = "too + adjective."

- **causar problemas** — "to cause problems."
