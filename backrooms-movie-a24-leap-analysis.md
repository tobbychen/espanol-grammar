# Analysis of The Backrooms Movie A24 Leap Article

## 1. Original Spanish Text

La serie de terror viral de Internet «The Backrooms», creada por el youtuber Kane Parsons, de 20 años, ha dado el salto al cine con gran éxito. Producida por el famoso estudio A24, la película ya ha batido récords de taquilla en el Reino Unido e Irlanda.

La producción fue muy intensa. Un pequeño equipo en Vancouver trabajó hasta el último minuto para terminar el proyecto. Este éxito pone de relieve una tendencia creciente entre los creadores digitales que dan el salto con éxito al cine y la televisión tradicionales. YouTube sirve como una plataforma muy accesible para cineastas con talento pero sin grandes presupuestos, donde pueden probar sus ideas y crear una comunidad antes de dar el salto a lo grande. Para los jóvenes creadores que empiezan en Internet, lo mejor es centrarse en el arte más que en el algoritmo de YouTube, encontrar un nicho específico y crear algo único que aún no exista. El apoyo de la familia, como la ayuda técnica con el hardware y la recuperación de datos por parte de padres con experiencia en el sector tecnológico, también juega un papel clave en estos comienzos independientes.

La película en sí explora entornos inquietantes y vacíos que fascinan al público porque despiertan recuerdos de la infancia, la lógica onírica y una extraña sensación de familiaridad. El universo más amplio de Backrooms está destinado a expandirse aún más, con más proyectos previstos para el futuro.

## 2. English Translation

The viral internet horror series "The Backrooms," created by 20-year-old YouTuber Kane Parsons, has made the leap to cinema with great success. Produced by the famous studio A24, the film has already broken box office records in the United Kingdom and Ireland.

The production was very intense. A small team in Vancouver worked until the last minute to finish the project. This success highlights a growing trend among digital creators who successfully make the leap to traditional film and television. YouTube serves as a very accessible platform for talented filmmakers without large budgets, where they can test their ideas and build a community before making the big leap. For young creators who are starting out on the internet, the best thing is to focus on the art rather than on the YouTube algorithm, find a specific niche, and create something unique that does not yet exist. Family support, such as technical help with hardware and data recovery from parents with experience in the tech sector, also plays a key role in these independent beginnings.

The film itself explores unsettling, empty environments that fascinate audiences because they awaken childhood memories, dreamlike logic, and a strange sense of familiarity. The broader Backrooms universe is destined to expand even further, with more projects planned for the future.

## 4. Grammar Analysis

### 4.1. The Present Perfect Tense (*pretérito perfecto compuesto*): *ha dado, ha batido*
The article opens with two present perfect constructions that report recent, present-relevant events:
- *ha **dado** el salto al cine* — "has made the leap to cinema"
- *ya ha **batido** récords* — "has already broken records"

Form: **haber (present) + past participle**: *ha dado* (has given), *ha batido* (has beaten). The present perfect is used here for events that have recently occurred and remain relevant to the current moment (the film is currently a success).

The article contrasts this with the preterite for events viewed as completed:
- *La producción **fue** muy intensa.* — "The production was very intense." (preterite of *ser*)

### 4.2. The Idiomatic Phrase **dar el salto**
The metaphorical expression **dar el salto** ("to make the leap / to take the plunge") is used three times in the article, with different complements:
- *ha **dado el salto** al cine* — has made the leap to cinema
- *que **dan el salto** con éxito al cine* — who successfully make the leap to cinema
- *antes de **dar el salto** a lo grande* — before making the big leap

This is a high-frequency idiomatic construction. *Dar* (to give) + *salto* (jump) means to take a major step forward, especially in a career. Compare with English "to take the plunge" or "to make the leap."

### 4.3. The Idiomatic Phrase **poner de relieve**
- *Este éxito **pone de relieve** una tendencia creciente* — "This success highlights a growing trend"

Another highly versatile Spanish idiom. Structure: *poner* (to put) + *de relieve* (in relief) = "to highlight, to bring to the fore, to emphasize." The literal image is "to put something in relief" (like a sculpture in bas-relief). The article uses this to introduce a broader observation from a specific event.

### 4.4. The Idiomatic Phrase **jugar un papel clave**
- *también **juega un papel clave** en estos comienzos* — "also plays a key role in these beginnings"

Structure: *jugar* (to play) + *un papel* (a role/paper) + adjective *clave* (key). This is a very common Spanish idiom meaning "to play a role / to have a function." The adjective *clave* (key) intensifies it. Synonyms: *desempeñar un papel* (to play a role, more formal), *tener un papel* (to have a role).

### 4.5. The Adverbial Phrase **a lo grande**
- *dar el salto **a lo grande*** — "to make the big leap"

This is a fixed adverbial phrase (locución adverbial) meaning "in a big way, in grand style." Structure: preposition *a* + neuter article *lo* + adjective *grande*. Compare with *a lo loco* (recklessly, in a crazy way), *a lo bestia* (beastly, in a rough way), *a lo grande* (grandly). The article uses this idiomatically to describe a major career transition.

### 4.6. The Construction **lo + adjective + ser** (Evaluative)
- ***lo mejor** es centrarse* — "the best thing is to focus"

Spanish uses the **neuter article *lo*** before adjectives (or adverbs) to form abstract noun phrases equivalent to English "the [adjective] thing" or "the [adjective] part":
- *lo mejor* = the best (thing)
- *lo peor* = the worst (thing)
- *lo importante* = the important (thing)
- *lo bueno* = the good (part)

This is a fundamental evaluative construction in Spanish and is essential for expressing opinions, advice, and judgments.

### 4.7. The Comparative with **más...que** in a Distributive Sense
- ***centrarse en el arte más que en** el algoritmo* — "to focus on the art rather than on the algorithm"

The structure *más...que* is normally comparative ("more X than Y"), but here it has a **distributive** function: "to do X rather than Y." This is similar to English "more X than Y" in the sense of "X rather than Y" (e.g., "I am more tired than hungry" = "I'm tired rather than hungry"). Spanish uses the same structure.

### 4.8. The Relative Clause with Future/Anticipated Reference: **que aún no exista**
- *crear algo único **que aún no exista*** — "to create something unique that does not yet exist"

Here the dependent verb *exista* is in the **present subjunctive** (*existir*, 3rd sg.) because it refers to a hypothetical or anticipated existence, not a current fact. The antecedent (*algo único*) is non-specific and prospective, which triggers the subjunctive mood. This is one of the classic uses of the subjunctive: in subordinate clauses referring to non-existent or hypothetical entities.

### 4.9. The Expression of Age: **de + number + años**
- *Kane Parsons, **de 20 años*** — "Kane Parsons, 20 years old"

Spanish expresses age with the formula: *tener [number] años* (to have X years) for a subject, and **de + number + años** as a postposed adjective phrase modifying a person. This is more compact than English "who is 20 years old."

### 4.10. The Preterite for Background Events
- *La producción **fue** muy intensa.* — "The production was very intense."
- *Un pequeño equipo... **trabajó** hasta el último minuto* — "A small team worked until the last minute"

The article uses the **preterite** (*fue, trabajó*) rather than the imperfect to describe past events that form a coherent narrative. The preterite is appropriate here because these are completed actions or states in the past, even though they were "ongoing" at some point — Spanish uses the preterite when the action is viewed as a whole event (vs. the imperfect, which emphasizes the duration or repetition of an action in progress).

### 4.11. The Neuter Use of **en sí**
- *La película **en sí** explora...* — "The film itself explores..."

The phrase **en sí** (in itself) uses the reflexive pronoun *sí* in its neuter form (referring back to the subject in a general, non-gendered way). It adds emphasis: "the film in and of itself" (as opposed to its broader context, such as the franchise or the cultural phenomenon). Compare with **de por sí** (in itself, by nature), **en sí mismo / en sí misma** (in itself, with gendered agreement).

### 4.12. The Construction **estar destinado a + infinitive**
- *está **destinado a expandirse** aún más* — "is destined to expand further"

Structure: *estar* (verb) + *destinado* (past participle of *destinar*, agreeing with the subject in gender/number) + preposition *a* + infinitive. This expresses a future that is fated or expected. Equivalent to English "to be bound to," "to be destined to," or "to be set to." A common construction for plans, predictions, and inevitabilities.

### 4.13. The Use of *-ante* and *-iente* Adjectives from Present Participles
The article uses several adjectives that are technically present participles of verbs, but functioning as adjectives:
- ***creciente*** — growing (from *crecer*)
- ***inquietantes*** — unsettling, disturbing (from *inquietar*)
- ***previstos*** — planned, foreseen (from *prever*)

These are formed with the **-ante** (for -ar verbs) or **-iente** (for -er/-ir verbs) suffix and behave as regular adjectives, agreeing in gender and number. They describe an ongoing or characteristic quality of the noun, not an action in progress.

## 5. Idioms and Expressions

### 5.1. **dar el salto** — "to make the leap / to take the plunge"
- *ha dado el salto al cine* — has made the leap to cinema
- *antes de dar el salto a lo grande* — before making the big leap
- A versatile idiom used for major transitions, especially in careers or life stages. The image is of a physical leap forward.

### 5.2. **batir récords** — "to break records"
- *ha batido récords de taquilla* — has broken box office records
- A high-frequency idiom in sports, business, and entertainment. *Batir* literally means "to beat, to whisk, to churn," so *batir un récord* is "to beat a record."

### 5.3. **poner de relieve** — "to highlight / to bring to the fore"
- *Este éxito pone de relieve una tendencia creciente* — This success highlights a growing trend
- A formal/literary idiom meaning "to emphasize, to make stand out." Compare with *destacar* (to highlight, more direct) and *subrayar* (to underline, more abstract).

### 5.4. **jugar un papel clave** — "to play a key role"
- *también juega un papel clave* — also plays a key role
- Synonyms: *desempeñar un papel fundamental*, *tener un rol importante*. The adjective *clave* (key) is common: *factor clave, momento clave, pieza clave* (key factor, key moment, key piece).

### 5.5. **a lo grande** — "in a big way / grandly"
- *dar el salto a lo grande* — to make the big leap
- An adverbial idiom of manner. Compare *a lo loco* (in a crazy way), *a lo bestia* (in a rough/beastly way). All use the pattern *a + lo + adjective* to create idiomatic adverbs of manner.

### 5.6. **lo mejor es** — "the best thing is"
- *lo mejor es centrarse en el arte* — the best thing is to focus on the art
- Part of the *lo + adjective* evaluative construction. Used for advice, judgments, and recommendations. Variants: *lo más importante es* (the most important thing is), *lo único que* (the only thing that).

### 5.7. **más que en** — "rather than"
- *centrarse en el arte más que en el algoritmo* — to focus on the art rather than the algorithm
- A distributive use of the comparative *más...que*. Distinguishes priorities, not necessarily quantities. Equivalent to English "more X than Y" in the sense of "X rather than Y."

### 5.8. **por parte de** — "from / on the part of"
- *por parte de padres con experiencia* — from parents with experience
- A prepositional phrase that identifies the source or agent of an action. *Por parte de* is a fixed idiom — it must always be used as a unit, not as separate words. Compare with *de parte de* (from — slightly different shade of meaning; often used in messages: "de parte de Juan" = "from John").

### 5.9. **estar destinado a** — "to be destined to"
- *está destinado a expandirse aún más* — is destined to expand even further
- Expresses a future that is expected or fated. The past participle *destinado* agrees with the subject. Common in journalism for upcoming events, products, or careers.

### 5.10. **despertar recuerdos** — "to awaken memories"
- *despiertan recuerdos de la infancia* — awaken memories of childhood
- A common collocation. *Despertar* (e→ie stem change) is "to wake, to awaken." The metaphorical use — "to awaken memories" — is very frequent in literary and journalistic Spanish.

### 5.11. **la lógica onírica** — "dreamlike logic"
- *la lógica onírica* — dreamlike logic
- An elevated expression combining the standard noun *lógica* (logic) with the adjective *onírica* (dream-related, from Greek *oneiros* = dream). The adjective ending **-ico/-ica** often carries this kind of elevated, technical, or specialized register.

### 5.12. **de 20 años** — "20 years old"
- *Kane Parsons, de 20 años* — Kane Parsons, 20 years old
- The standard formula for expressing age as a postposed adjective. Compare with the predicate form: *tiene 20 años* (he is 20 years old, literally "he has 20 years").

### 5.13. **previstos para el futuro** — "planned for the future"
- *más proyectos previstos para el futuro* — more projects planned for the future
- A common collocation in journalism and planning contexts. *Previsto* is the past participle of *prever* (to foresee, to anticipate), and the phrase *previsto para* (planned for / scheduled for) is used for upcoming events, releases, or projects.

### 5.14. **de cine** / **al cine** — "to cinema / cinematic"
- *ha dado el salto al cine* — has made the leap to cinema
- The preposition *a* + article *el* (contracted to *al*) + noun *cine* (cinema) is a directional phrase: "to the cinema." *Cine* is also used adjectivally: *una película de cine* (a movie theater film, as opposed to streaming).
