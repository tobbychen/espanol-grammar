# Spanish Grammar Rules

This document outlines basic Spanish grammar rules for nouns and phrases.

## Nouns

Spanish nouns have a gender (masculine or feminine) and a number (singular or plural).

### Gender

- **Masculine:** Nouns that typically end in **-o** are masculine.
  - *el libro* (the book)
  - *el niño* (the boy)
- **Feminine:** Nouns that typically end in **-a** are feminine.
  - *la casa* (the house)
  - *la niña* (the girl)

There are exceptions to these rules.

### Number

- To make a noun plural, add **-s** if it ends in a vowel.
  - *libros*, *casas*
- Add **-es** if it ends in a consonant.
  - *el color* -> *los colores*

## Phrases

A phrase is a group of words that work together in a sentence.

### Prepositional Phrases

A prepositional phrase consists of a preposition followed by a noun or pronoun. It can function as an adjective or an adverb.

- **Example:** `con satisfacción`
  - `con` (preposition) + `satisfacción` (noun)
  - This phrase acts as an adverb, describing *how* an action is done.

### Subclauses

A subclause (or subordinate clause) is a part of a sentence that has its own subject and verb but cannot stand alone as a complete sentence. They are often introduced by conjunctions like `que`, `cuando`, `si`, etc.

- **Example:** `El libro **que me diste** es interesante.`
  - The subclause is "que me diste" (that you gave me).
  - It modifies "El libro" and has a subject (tú, implied) and a verb (diste).
